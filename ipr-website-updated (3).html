Step 03 — Act

Step-by-Step Filing
Guide (India)

Your complete roadmap from creation to legal protection.

1
Conduct a Comprehensive Trademark Search
Search the IP India database for identical and deceptively similar marks across all 45 Nice Classification classes. Run both phonetic and visual similarity checks. A thorough search prevents costly oppositions — in Cadila Health Care v. Cadila Pharmaceuticals (2001), the Supreme Court established a multi-factor test for deceptive similarity, including phonetic and visual resemblance, the nature of goods, and consumer literacy.
ipo.gov.in → TM Search
2
Assess Mark Distinctiveness
Marks fall on a spectrum: Generic (never registrable) → Descriptive (must prove acquired distinctiveness) → Suggestive → Arbitrary → Fanciful (strongest). The stronger the mark, the easier enforcement. Invented words like "KODAK" or "XEROX" get the widest protection. Avoid descriptive marks — the Registry will likely raise a Section 9 objection under the Trade Marks Act, 1999.
TM Act S.9 — Absolute Grounds
3
Classify Your Goods / Services (Nice Classification)
Identify the correct Nice Classification class(es) out of 45 (Classes 1–34: goods; 35–45: services). Each class requires a separate fee. Incorrect classification can result in refusal or gaps in protection. For a food-tech startup, you may need Class 30 (food), Class 35 (business services), and Class 43 (restaurant services) simultaneously.
Nice Classification (1–45)
4
File Form TM-A Online
File at ipindiaonline.gov.in. Include: mark representation (word/device/composite), class, goods/services description, applicant details, date of first use (if claiming prior use). Govt. fee: ₹4,500 (individual/startup/MSME) or ₹9,000 (company). You get TM-filing date as your priority date from this moment — critical for first-to-file priority. Use ™ symbol immediately after filing.
ipindiaonline.gov.in
5
Respond to the Examination Report
The Examiner issues a report within 30 days. You have 2 months to respond to objections (extendable). Common objections: lack of distinctiveness (S.9), deceptive similarity to existing marks (S.11), or descriptive nature. Prepare a counter-statement with evidence of use, distinctiveness, and market reputation. If objection is not resolved, a personal hearing is scheduled before the Registrar.
Response window: 2 months
6
Publication in the Trade Marks Journal
Approved marks are published in the official Trade Marks Journal for a 4-month opposition window. Any person can file an opposition under S.21 citing prior rights, bad faith, or likelihood of confusion. Both sides exchange evidence and are heard. If unopposed — or if the opposition fails — the mark proceeds directly to registration. Monitor the Journal for conflicting third-party applications too.
Opposition window: 4 months
7
Certificate of Registration + Maintenance
Registration certificate issued and valid for 10 years from filing date, renewable indefinitely every 10 years (Form TM-R). Use ® symbol only after registration. Failure to use the mark for 5 consecutive years exposes it to cancellation under S.47 (non-use). Maintain evidence of continuous commercial use — invoices, advertising records, product packaging. Consider international protection via the Madrid Protocol as you scale.
Total: 18–24 months
1
Creation = Automatic Protection (But Register Anyway)
Copyright arises automatically upon original creation fixed in material form — no registration or formality required under S.13, Copyright Act, 1957. However, registration creates a public record, acts as prima facie evidence of ownership in court, and is essential for filing an infringement suit quickly. The Supreme Court in Eastern Book Company v. D.B. Modak (2008) held that mere effort isn't enough — the work must reflect the author's creative choices. A "modicum of creativity" is required. Unregistered works can still be enforced, but registration shifts the burden of proof in your favour.
Copyright Act, 1957 — S.13
2
Understand What Copyright Protects (and What It Doesn't)
Copyright protects the expression of ideas — not the ideas themselves (the idea-expression dichotomy under S.13). Protected works: literary works (including source code), dramatic works, musical works, artistic works, cinematographic films, and sound recordings. Not protected: titles, slogans, names, facts, or news items. For software, both source code and object code are protected as "literary works." For databases, the selection and arrangement must reflect creativity — raw data alone doesn't qualify.
Idea-Expression Dichotomy
3
Document Your Authorship Rigorously
Build a paper trail: version-controlled commits with timestamps (GitHub/GitLab), email drafts, signed agreements with collaborators, dated sketches or manuscripts. For software: both source and object code. For music: original recordings with metadata. For art: physical originals + high-resolution scans. In joint works, clearly document each co-author's contribution — S.2(z) defines joint authorship and ownership is shared. If created during employment, check if S.17 (employer as first owner) applies.
Keep timestamped evidence
4
File Form XIV at copyright.gov.in
Submit online or by post to the Copyright Office (New Delhi). Fee: ₹500 per literary/dramatic/musical/artistic work; ₹2,000 for cinematographic films; ₹500 for sound recordings. Include: title, nature of work, author details, year of creation, two copies of the work. File separate applications for each distinct work. If the work is unpublished, mark it so — it still qualifies. Assignments and licences must be in writing under S.19 and S.30 respectively.
copyright.gov.in
5
Mandatory 30-Day Objection Window
After filing, the Copyright Office issues a mandatory diary number and waits 30 days for third-party objections under S.45. If an objection is filed, both parties are heard before the Registrar of Copyrights. The examiner scrutinises the application and may raise discrepancies (wrong author name, unclear work description, missing NOC from publisher). If no objection is raised and the examiner is satisfied, the work proceeds to registration. This is not a "registered mark" — it is an "entry in the Register of Copyrights."
Objection window: 30 days
6
Entry in Register of Copyrights + Ongoing Rights
Certificate of registration issued. Duration: Life + 60 years for most works (S.22); 60 years from publication for anonymous/pseudonymous works, posthumous works, and government/corporate works (S.27–29). The register is publicly searchable at copyright.gov.in. Registration aids licensing, assignment, and in customs seizure of infringing imports. Moral rights (right of paternity and integrity under S.57) exist separately from economic rights and cannot be waived by contract.
Total: 1–3 months
1
Prior Art Search — Before Everything Else
Search IPO (ipindiapatents.gov.in), USPTO, EPO (Espacenet), and Google Patents. Your invention must be: (a) novel — not disclosed anywhere in the world before; (b) non-obvious — not obvious to a "person skilled in the art"; (c) industrially applicable. Check S.3 of the Patents Act, 1970 for what is NOT patentable — including mathematical methods, business methods, mere discoveries, and computer programs "per se." The landmark Novartis AG v. Union of India (2013) case reinforced that incremental innovations without enhanced therapeutic efficacy (the S.3(d) bar) cannot be patented. Document all prior art found.
ipindiapatents.gov.in
2
Determine Inventorship and Ownership
The inventor is the natural person(s) who conceived the inventive concept — not who built or funded it. Under S.6, an application can be filed by the inventor, their assignee, or legal representative. For employer-employee situations: inventions made during employment and in the course of duties belong to the employer (S.82 analogy via contract). Ensure written IP assignment from all co-inventors before filing. Joint inventors must each sign the declaration. Incorrect inventorship can invalidate a patent post-grant.
Inventorship ≠ Ownership
3
Draft Patent Specification (Complete or Provisional)
A provisional specification secures your priority date with a basic description — you have 12 months to file the complete specification (Form 2). A complete specification includes: title, field of invention, background/prior art, detailed description, drawings (if applicable), abstract, and most critically — claims. Claims define the exact legal boundary of your patent rights. Draft broadly (independent claims) with narrower fallback positions (dependent claims). Hire a registered patent agent (mandatory for foreign applicants; recommended for Indian individuals too) for complex inventions.
Claims = most critical section
4
File Provisional Application (Form 1 + 2)
File at the appropriate Patent Office (Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai, or Kolkata — based on applicant's location). Fees: ₹1,600 (individual/startup), ₹4,000 (small entity), ₹8,000 (large entity). For international protection, file a PCT application within 12 months of your priority date to enter 150+ countries through a single filing. Include a Statement of Undertaking (Form 3) disclosing corresponding applications abroad. If the invention relates to defence/atomic energy, file only in India first under S.39.
Provisional → 12 months → Complete
5
Automatic Publication at 18 Months
Your application is automatically published in the Official Gazette of the Patent Office 18 months from the priority date. This makes your invention public. Before publication, you cannot enforce rights but also cannot lose novelty due to your own disclosures after filing. To accelerate publication (e.g., for licensing negotiations or enforcement), file a Request for Early Publication (Form 9) — the application is published within 1 month of request. Note: Publication does not mean grant.
Early: File Form 9
6
Request Examination (Form 18 or 18A)
Examination is NOT automatic — you must file Form 18 (regular) or Form 18A (expedited) within 48 months from the priority date or face abandonment. Expedited examination (Form 18A) is available to: startups, MSMEs, individual inventors, and applicants with PCT national phase. The examiner issues a First Examination Report (FER) citing prior art and objections. You must respond within 6 months (extendable to 9 months). Multiple rounds of examination (FER-reply cycles) are possible. Pre-grant opposition can be filed by any person after publication under S.25(1).
Fee: ₹4,000 (individual)
7
Patent Grant, Post-Grant Opposition & Annual Renewal
Once examination objections are resolved, the patent is sealed and granted. It is published in the Official Gazette. From grant, any person may file a post-grant opposition (S.25(2)) within 12 months at the Patent Office. Pay annual renewal fees starting from the 3rd year after filing — miss one and the patent lapses (restoration possible under S.60 within 18 months). Total protection: up to 20 years from filing date. Working statements (Form 27) must be filed annually to confirm commercial use in India — compulsory licensing (S.84) can be triggered if the patent is not worked.
Total: 3–5 years
1
Establish the Geographic Origin–Quality Link
Under S.1(3)(e) of the GI Act, 1999, a GI is a sign used on goods that originate from a specific geographic region and that possess qualities, reputation, or characteristics essentially attributable to that origin. Document: (a) historical evidence of production in the region, (b) the specific quality or characteristic that derives from the geography (soil, climate, traditional process), and (c) the community of producers. This "link" is the foundation of your application — unlike a trademark, the IP belongs to the region, not a person. In Tea Board India v. ITC Ltd. (2010), the Calcutta HC explored how GI rights can protect geographic names even outside the registered product category.
GI Act, 1999 — S.11
2
Form an Eligible Producers' Association
Only an "association of persons," producers' organisation, or any authority established by law can apply — not individual producers. Under S.11, the applicant must represent the interests of the entire producer community in that region. Establish a formal association with: registered bylaws, a defined membership list (all producers in the region), democratic governance structure, and a mechanism to grant "authorised user" status to individual producers. Ensure the association has legal standing (registered society or cooperative is ideal).
Collective right only
3
Draft and File Form GI-1 at the GI Registry, Chennai
Submit to the Geographical Indications Registry (Trade Marks Registry, Chennai). The application must include: a statement of case establishing the origin link, class of goods (under Schedule 1 of GI Rules), the defined geographic area (with a map), proof of quality/reputation (scientific reports, historical records, surveys), production standards, and inspection structures. Fee: ₹5,000. For handicrafts, coordinate with the Office of Development Commissioner (Handicrafts). The Registry assigns a GI number upon receipt.
ipindia.gov.in → GI Registry
4
Examination, GI Journal Publication & Opposition
The Examiner reviews the application — scrutinising the geographic-quality link, completeness of documentation, and whether the GI is already generic. If accepted, the GI is published in the Geographical Indications Journal. Any person may file a Notice of Opposition within 3 months of publication, citing: the GI is misleading, generic, contrary to public order, or conflicts with existing IP rights. Both parties file evidence and counter-statements. The Registrar issues a final decision. This process can take 12–18 months from filing.
Opposition: 3 months
5
Enrol Authorised Users (Form GI-2)
Individual producers within the region must separately register as "authorised users" under S.17 (Form GI-2, fee: ₹500). Only authorised users can use the GI tag commercially on their products. The registered applicant (producers' association) manages the authorised user registry and sets quality standards. Infringement of a registered GI carries criminal penalties (up to 3 years + ₹50,000 fine under S.39) and civil remedies. Well-known Indian GIs: Darjeeling Tea (first GI registered in India, 2004), Kanchipuram Silk, Kolhapuri Chappal, Basmati Rice, Alphonso Mango, and 600+ others.
Renewable every 10 years
Step 04 — Avoid

10 IP Mistakes That
Kill Great Ideas

Most IP failures aren't bad ideas — they're avoidable errors in timing, paperwork, and strategy.

Mistake 01
Public Disclosure Before Patent Filing
Presenting your invention at a conference, posting on social media, or publishing before filing destroys novelty and makes your invention unpatentable.
File a provisional application before any public disclosure — even a casual LinkedIn post counts.
Mistake 02
Assuming Copyright = Trademark
Your logo is auto-protected by copyright, but that won't stop someone from launching a competing brand with a similar name. Only trademark gives exclusive commercial identity rights.
Register both: copyright for the artistic work, trademark for brand exclusivity in commerce.
Mistake 03
Choosing a Generic or Descriptive Mark
"Best Bread" or "Fast Delivery" cannot be registered. The Trade Marks Act requires distinctiveness. Generic marks can never be registered.
Choose an invented, arbitrary, or suggestive mark. Test distinctiveness before investing in branding.
Mistake 04
No IP Clause in Employment Agreements
Without explicit IP assignment clauses, ownership of employee-created work is disputed. Default rules may not favour the employer in all cases.
Include IP assignment, confidentiality, and non-compete clauses in every employment contract.
Mistake 05
Verbal Licensing Agreements
Under Section 19 of the Copyright Act, assignment must be in writing and signed. Oral terms on royalties and scope are nearly impossible to enforce.
Always document licenses: scope, duration, territory, royalty rate, exclusivity, sublicensing rights.
Mistake 06
No International Protection Strategy
An Indian trademark or patent gives zero protection abroad. Competitors in export markets can freely use your brand or copy your patented technology.
Use Madrid Protocol (trademark) and PCT (patent) to file internationally as you scale.
Mistake 07
Not Monitoring or Enforcing Rights
Trademark rights can be lost through non-enforcement ("genericide"). Allowing infringers to operate unchallenged weakens your legal position over time.
Set up watch services, Google Alerts, and enforce consistently — even against small infringers.
Mistake 08
Sharing Trade Secrets Without NDAs
India has no standalone trade secrets statute. Protection depends entirely on contract law. Sharing formulas or algorithms without NDAs leaves zero legal recourse.
Sign NDAs before sharing any confidential info — with investors, partners, employees, and vendors.
Mistake 09
Missing Renewal and Annuity Deadlines
Trademarks expire every 10 years. Patent annual fees are due each year after grant. Missing even one deadline can cause irreversible lapse of your IP rights.
Maintain a dedicated IP calendar with all renewal dates and 90-day advance reminders.
Mistake 10
GPL Open Source in Commercial Products
Incorporating GPL-licensed code in commercial software can force you to release your entire codebase publicly — the "copyleft" viral effect that has destroyed startup value.
Audit all dependencies. Use MIT or Apache 2.0 licensed libraries in commercial products only.
Step 05 — Learn

Landmark Cases
Every Creator Must Know

Real Indian IP cases — the legal lessons that shaped modern practice.

Trademark1996 · Supreme Court
N.R. Dongre v. Whirlpool Corporation
Whirlpool obtained an injunction against an Indian company using "Whirlpool" — despite no Indian registration — based on its transborder reputation as a well-known international mark.
⚖ A well-known foreign mark can be enforced in India without local registration. Reputation crosses borders before paperwork does.
Patent2013 · Supreme Court
Novartis AG v. Union of India
Novartis was denied a patent for a modified form of its cancer drug Gleevec. The Supreme Court upheld Section 3(d), blocking "evergreening" — minor modifications without enhanced therapeutic efficacy.
⚖ India blocks pharmaceutical companies from patenting incremental changes. Access to medicine beats monopoly extension.
Copyright2008 · Supreme Court
Eastern Book Company v. D.B. Modak
Court judgments require a "modicum of creativity" for copyright. Mere copy-editing was insufficient. India rejected the "sweat of the brow" doctrine — effort alone creates no copyright.
⚖ Copyright requires creativity, not just labour. Hard work alone doesn't create IP rights in India.
Trademark · Domain1999 · Delhi HC
Yahoo! Inc. v. Akash Arora
India's first cybersquatting case. Yahoo! stopped "yahooindia.com" — holding that domain names are protectable as trademarks under passing-off principles, even without express domain-specific registration.
⚖ Domain names are trademark assets. Register across all major TLDs before competitors do.
Design + Trademark2018 · Delhi HC
Carlsberg Breweries v. Som Distilleries
Carlsberg asserted both trademark and trade dress rights against a confusingly similar bottle design and label — showing how multiple IP types can protect different aspects of one product.
⚖ Stack your IP: trademark + design + copyright can simultaneously protect different dimensions of one product.
GI2010 · Calcutta HC
Tea Board India v. ITC Limited
Tea Board challenged ITC naming a hotel lounge "Darjeeling," arguing it misled consumers into linking it with GI-protected Darjeeling tea — exploring GI rights beyond product categories.
⚖ GI protection can extend beyond products to prevent misuse of geographic names in unrelated service industries.
Trademark2001 · Supreme Court
Cadila Health Care v. Cadila Pharmaceuticals
The Supreme Court laid down a comprehensive multi-factor test for "deceptive similarity" between pharmaceutical marks — considering phonetic, visual, and structural resemblance, plus the literacy of the consumer and the seriousness of confusion in medicinal products.
⚖ In pharma trademarks, the standard of similarity is stricter — even a small chance of consumer confusion can be fatal to a brand.
Copyright · Fair Dealing2016 · Delhi HC
The Chancellor, Masters & Scholars of the University of Oxford v. Rameshwari Photocopy Services
Delhi University's photocopy shop was sued by Oxford, Cambridge, and other publishers for copying textbook excerpts for students. The Delhi HC held that such reproduction for educational purposes fell within the "fair dealing" exception under S.52(1)(i) of the Copyright Act.
⚖ Educational reproduction is a permitted fair dealing under Indian copyright law — but only for courses, not commercial distribution.
Patent · Compulsory License2012 · IPAB
Bayer Corporation v. NATCO Pharma
India's first compulsory licence was granted to NATCO Pharma to manufacture a generic version of Bayer's patented cancer drug Nexavar. The IPAB found Bayer had not worked the patent in India and the drug was unaffordable — both triggering S.84 of the Patents Act.
⚖ A patent is a social contract. Failure to make the invention affordable and accessible in India can open the door to compulsory licensing.
Trademark · Well-Known Mark2010 · Delhi HC
Tata Sons Ltd. v. Manoj Dodia
The Delhi HC declared "TATA" a well-known trademark under S.11(6) of the Trade Marks Act, 1999, recognising its extraordinary reputation across diverse goods and services. This gave Tata protection across all classes, not just those for which it was registered.
⚖ A "well-known mark" under Indian law receives cross-class protection. Building brand reputation is its own IP strategy.
Copyright · Performers' Rights2006 · Delhi HC
Anil Gupta v. Kunal Dasgupta
The plaintiff claimed copyright over the concept of a reality television show about matchmaking. The Delhi HC held that while mere ideas aren't copyrightable, a detailed concept note with unique formats and structures could receive protection as a literary work.
⚖ A well-documented concept note or show bible can be copyrighted. Ideas can't be owned — but the detailed expression of them can.
Trade Secret · Contract2019 · Bombay HC
Bombay Dyeing v. Nusli Wadia (Trade Secret Context)
Indian courts have consistently held that in the absence of a specific trade secrets statute, protection flows from contract law and breach of confidence. NDAs and employment agreements are the primary legal shields — courts will grant injunctions to prevent disclosure of confidential business information misappropriated by former employees or partners.
⚖ India has no dedicated trade secrets law. Your only protection is a well-drafted NDA, confidentiality clause, and breach of contract action.

Licensing & Monetization

3 Ways to Make IP Pay

Your IP registration is just the start.

🤝
Exclusive License
One licensee gets sole rights in a territory or field. Higher upfront payments typical. You retain ownership but cannot license to others in that scope.
Best for: Premium partnerships, entering new markets through a single strong partner
📡
Non-Exclusive License
License the same IP to multiple parties simultaneously. Each pays royalties. You retain full ownership and maximum commercial flexibility.
Best for: Software platforms, tech that scales across industries without cannibalisation
🔄
Cross-License
Two companies grant each other access to their respective IP portfolios. Common in tech where products overlap patents held by multiple players.
Best for: Avoiding litigation, joint ventures, tech stack integration with partners
Startup-Specific Mistakes
🚀
Launching without an IP audit
A cease-and-desist after launch is catastrophic. Check if your product name, logo, and core technology infringe existing IP before going public — not after raising funds.
👥
No IP assignment from co-founders
IP created before company incorporation may belong personally to the founder. If they leave or turn hostile, they could own your startup's core technology.
💼
Undervaluing IP in investor negotiations
IP is a bankable asset. Not quantifying your registered IP portfolio often leads to significant undervaluation in term sheets and due diligence.
🌍
Only protecting IP in India while going global
Indian filings protect you only within India. Competitors abroad can freely use your brand name or patented method with zero legal consequences.
🔓
No IP assignment clause for contractors
Work created by freelancers doesn't automatically belong to you. Under Indian copyright law, the creator owns it unless a written assignment exists.

India uses the term "fair dealing" (Section 52, Copyright Act, 1957) — not "fair use" (which is the American term). Fair dealing is a closed list of permitted acts: research, private study, criticism, review, reporting current events, judicial proceedings, and educational use. The landmark Delhi HC case University of Oxford v. Rameshwari Photocopy Services (2016) held that photocopying textbook excerpts for university courses qualifies as fair dealing. Unlike the US, India does not have a flexible four-factor fair use test — if your use isn't on the S.52 list, it's likely infringement.

If someone misappropriates your trade secret, you can sue for breach of contract, breach of confidence, and seek injunction + damages.
